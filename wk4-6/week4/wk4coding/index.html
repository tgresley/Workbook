first p5 sketches

I clearly didn’t go very realistic on the portrait, did I? Still adjusting to p5, I decided to just make it abstract and use basic shapes and colours to create a face—if not mine. I also experimented with animating a line of ellipses to follow the cursor, creating an interactive drawing element. The ability to add interaction into a page that’s more complex than what I was previously able to do is exciting!

As we were encouraged to take inspiration from the Yayoi Kusama exhibition we visited in class, I created a simple flower shape (since we saw so many of them) and randomly generated polka dots on top. The addition of randomness that p5 offers is also very exciting in its ability to foster interaction.

I started by experimenting with basic colour and shape in my first p5 sketches. Transitioning into the visual language of drawing through code was unexpectedly refreshing—especially after finding HTML and CSS styling to be counterintuitive and frustrating. With p5.js, the process felt more intuitive and creative, allowing me to generate personal imagery more naturally, rather than relying on finding and inserting JPEGs.

These are the progress screenshots from my Week 4 landing page. I initially struggled to use p5 to create a grid that could generate a pattern of cells. After figuring that out, I also had to experiment with how changing the background and adding elements in HTML interacted with the p5 sketch I created. What I found most difficult was how the two canvases—HTML and p5—layered on top of each other, and how to get all the elements to stack correctly.
